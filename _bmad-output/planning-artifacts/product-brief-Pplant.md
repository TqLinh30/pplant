# Product Brief: Pplant

## Executive Summary

Pplant is a personal planner for students who want to understand and improve how they use both money and time. Many students track spending in one place, tasks in another, and part-time work hours somewhere else, if at all. The result is a fragmented picture: they may know they spent too much this month, but not whether that spending happened on free days, after missed tasks, during stressful weeks, or before they understood how many hours of work those expenses represented.

Pplant brings expense tracking, daily planning, reminders, and student work-income awareness into one lightweight assistant. Its core promise is simple: help students see what they earn, where their money goes, how their time is being used, and how free time affects spending habits. Instead of behaving like a strict finance dashboard, Pplant should feel like a gentle companion that nudges users toward better daily choices.

## The Problem

Students often face three connected problems at once: they do not know where their money goes, they overspend before noticing the pattern, and they forget or delay work they intended to finish. Existing expense trackers can show categories and budgets, while task apps can manage reminders, but students still have to mentally connect their money decisions with their daily rhythm.

This is especially painful for students who earn part-time income. A coffee, meal, ride, or impulse purchase is not just an amount; it also represents time worked. Without a clear view of "how much I earned per hour," "how many hours I worked," and "how much of that was spent," students struggle to build a practical sense of financial discipline.

## The Solution

Pplant combines a student-focused expense tracker with a daily planner. Users can record income and expenses manually or by taking a receipt photo that the app automatically parses, organize spending by category and topic, set a monthly budget, and review spending by day, week, and month. Alongside money tracking, users can plan daily tasks, set priorities, track progress through To Do, Doing, and Done, add deadlines, and create repeat reminders.

The daily loop should be lightweight: open a combined home overview, plan the day, record money as it is spent, check reminders, mark work done, and review the relationship between free time, earned income, spending, and savings. The product should avoid shame-based messaging. It should nudge students with calm, practical feedback that helps them feel more in control.

The key product insight is that spending and time are not separate behaviors. Pplant should help users answer questions like:

- How much did I earn this week, and how many hours did it take?
- Which categories took the most money this month?
- How much of my budget is left?
- Did I spend more on days when I had more free time?
- How much did I save compared with my plan?

## Who This Serves

The primary user is a student who wants better control over everyday life without adopting a complex finance system. This user may receive allowance, earn part-time income, or combine both. They need fast capture, friendly reminders, and clear summaries more than advanced financial tools.

Early adopters are likely to be students who already feel some anxiety about spending or productivity but have not found a simple system that sticks. Their "aha moment" comes when Pplant translates money into time and habit: they can finally see not only what they spent, but what that spending meant in hours worked and daily behavior.

## What Makes This Different

Most expense apps emphasize transaction capture, categories, budgets, and reports. Planner apps emphasize tasks, deadlines, and reminders. Pplant's differentiation is the connection between the two: it treats money, free time, work hours, and daily discipline as one student-life system.

The first version does not need bank integrations or advanced automation to be valuable. Its advantage is focus: a lightweight tool designed around the student question, "Am I using my time and money in a way that supports the life I want?"

## MVP Scope

The first version should include:

- Income and expense recording
- Manual transaction entry
- Receipt photo capture with automatic receipt parsing for expense entry
- Spending categories and topics
- Monthly budget tracking
- Spending statistics by category, topic, day, week, and month
- Remaining budget summary
- Savings summary
- Work-income tracking through direct entry of hours worked and hourly wage
- Shift-based work entry, where students can enter work time and apply a configured hourly wage
- Daily task planner
- Task states: To Do, Doing, Done
- Task priority: high and low
- Deadlines
- Repeat reminders
- Combined home overview for money, tasks, reminders, budget status, and savings progress

The first version should explicitly exclude:

- Bank account linking
- Investment tracking
- Debt management
- Full family or group budgeting
- Complex financial forecasting

## Success Criteria

Pplant is working if students use it repeatedly as part of their daily routine, not only when they feel guilty about spending. Early success should be measured by:

- Number of expenses recorded per active user per week
- Number of planned tasks completed per active user per week
- Percentage of users who set a monthly budget
- Percentage of users who review weekly or monthly summaries
- Number of users who record work hours and hourly income
- User-reported clarity: "I understand where my money and time went this week"
- User-reported behavior change: "I spent less impulsively" or "I planned my day better"

## Vision

In two to three years, Pplant can become an intelligent planner that combines money, time, and habits for young people. It can evolve from a manual tracker into a proactive assistant that recognizes patterns, suggests better routines, warns when spending is drifting, and helps students connect effort, income, free time, and goals.

The long-term opportunity is not just another budgeting app or task manager. It is a student-life operating system: gentle, practical, and personal enough to help users build financial awareness and daily discipline before adulthood makes those lessons more expensive.
